I've already delivered the expanded 20-session syllabus in my previous message. Here it is again in case you missed it — two sessions for every original week, labeled Week N of 20:

Session 1 — Week 1 of 20
Project kickoff & concept: introduce the 2-wheel warehouse robot, look at real warehouse robots (Amazon Kiva, etc.), define scope, success criteria, and what "done" looks like. Set up Python + pygame on the student's machine.

Session 2 — Week 2 of 20
First Python simulation: build a keyboard-controlled robot on a blank canvas — forward motion and rotation with arrow keys, screen wrap or wall bounds. Commit v0 to GitHub.

Session 3 — Week 3 of 20
Robot logic concepts: walk through the simplest navigation rule (move forward, rotate when blocked). Whiteboard the state machine: MOVING → BLOCKED → ROTATING → MOVING. Add a fake obstacle in the simulation and implement the rule in Python.

Session 4 — Week 4 of 20
Electronics basics: hands-on tour of motors, servos, distance sensors, microcontroller, motor driver, and battery. What each one does, how they connect, voltage/current basics, and why a motor driver exists (microcontroller can't power motors directly).

Session 5 — Week 5 of 20
How distance sensors work: TOF vs IR — light, time-of-flight, reflection, range, blind spots, failure modes (mirrors, dark surfaces, sunlight). Read a TOF datasheet together and pick which one we'll use.

Session 6 — Week 6 of 20
Differential drive math: derive forward = both wheels same speed, rotate = wheels opposite, arc = wheels different speeds. Work out left/right wheel speeds for a target turn, then plug the formulas into the Python sim so the simulated robot turns like the real one will.

Session 7 — Week 7 of 20
Software setup: install Arduino IDE, add the ESP32-S3 board package, upload Blink, confirm Serial Monitor works. Troubleshoot driver / port issues so the rest of the build is painless.

Session 8 — Week 8 of 20
Safe wiring: lay out the chassis, wire battery → motor driver → motors, wire ESP32-S3 → motor driver control pins, wire the distance sensor. Power-on checklist, polarity, common short-circuit mistakes. First "it lights up and doesn't smoke" moment.

Session 9 — Week 9 of 20
Basic motion — straight line: write the first motor-control sketch. Drive forward slow, reverse slow, stop. Tune PWM duty cycle so the robot actually moves in a straight line (one motor is always slightly faster — calibrate).

Session 10 — Week 10 of 20
Basic motion — turning: rotate in place, approximate a 10° / 45° / 90° turn using timed wheel motion. Measure actual vs intended angle, log the error, and build a small turn-angle lookup table.

Session 11 — Week 11 of 20
Live sensor reads: stream distance values into the Serial Monitor, plot them, see noise. Add simple smoothing (rolling average) so the readings are usable.

Session 12 — Week 12 of 20
Feedback peripherals: wire and code an LED + buzzer. Map robot states to feedback — green LED moving, red LED blocked, short beep on turn start. Now the robot tells you what it "thinks" without a laptop attached.

Session 13 — Week 13 of 20
Build the test environment: cut and tape together a cardboard warehouse track — straight aisles, a couple of corners, a dead end. Decide what counts as "successful navigation."

Session 14 — Week 14 of 20
Closed-loop navigation on the track: combine distance sensing + motion rule + feedback peripherals. Robot should drive the track, stop before walls, rotate, and continue. Iterate on threshold distances and turn timings until it makes a full loop.

Session 15 — Week 15 of 20
Servo-mounted scanner: mount the distance sensor on a servo, sweep left / front / right, log three readings per cycle. Now the robot can "see" before committing to a turn direction.

Session 16 — Week 16 of 20
SLAM concept day + kits overview: whiteboard what SLAM is (Simultaneous Localization And Mapping) — no implementation, just the intuition (landmarks, pose, map). Survey real robot kits (TurtleBot, Pololu, etc.) and where ours sits.

Session 17 — Week 17 of 20
Lightweight mapping in Python: build an internal grid (2D array) and a pose (x, y, heading). Every time the robot moves or turns, update the grid. Run the sim and the real robot side by side and log the path.

Session 18 — Week 18 of 20
Visualize + debug: render the grid as a top-down map with the path drawn over it. Reliability day — fix the three flakiest things (battery sag, sensor noise, drift on turns). Pick upgrades worth doing in the last two sessions.

Session 19 — Week 19 of 20
Final integration: full warehouse-path run end to end, twice. Tighten anything still loose. Push final code to GitHub, write the README, capture GIFs of the robot navigating, and draft the resume blurb together.

Session 20 — Week 20 of 20
Demo Day: student presents — problem, design choices, Python simulation, hardware build, results, and what they'd do next. Live robot run for the audience.

Time consistency: why Update() frame rate varies across desktop / mobile / headset, and how Time.deltaTime makes movement device-independent. Worked example: speed × deltaTime in MotorMgr.Rotate.

People (PeopleMgr.cs): spawning NPCs, detecting when a person is out of bounds, near the robot, or near another person, and triggering a rotate-then-move response.

Session 15 — Week 15 of 20
